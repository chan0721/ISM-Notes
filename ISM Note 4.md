# 2. Radiative process


First of all, we need to define a quantity called Intensity. The intensity is defined by $I_{\nu} = \frac{dE}{dA dt d\Omega d\nu}$, which is the energy per unit area per unit time per unit solid angle per unit frequency, the unit of which is $erg/s/cm^2/Hz/steradian$. Here the $d \Omega$ and $d A$ are not related since usually the radiation is not a point source. Then we can introduce the flux, which is defined by $ F_{\nu} = \int I_{\nu} \cos \theta \, d\Omega $, and its unit is $erg/s/cm^2/Hz$. Then straightforwardly, we can define the mean intensity by $J_{\nu} = \frac{1}{4\pi} \int I_{\nu} d\Omega$, here we don't have the $\cos \theta$ term since we don't need to consider the direction of the radiation. After that, we can define the energy density by $u_{\nu} = \frac{4\pi}{c} J_{\nu}$, it is the angular integrated flux/c, the unit here is $erg/cm^3/Hz$.


The next question is, for different kind of gas, what is the energy density? WE assume the gas is in thermal equilibrium and is ideal gas. The question is , what is the possibility of finding the small system at energy E? The answer is given by the Boltzmann distribution, which is $P(E) \propto e^{-E/kT}$, while for idealized gas the energy is given by $E = \frac{1}{2} m v^2$, so the velocity distribution is given by $P(\vec{v}) \propto e^{-m|\vec{v}|^2/2kT}$. Then we can introduce the distribution function $f(v) = n \left( \frac{m}{2\pi kT} \right)^{3/2} e^{-mv^2/2kT}$, where $n = \int f(\vec{v}) d^3 \vec{v}$. And if we change the variable from vector to scalar, we can get the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution, which is $f(v) = 4\pi v^2 n \left( \frac{m}{2\pi kT} \right)^{3/2} e^{-mv^2/2kT}$. The mean velocity is given by $\langle v \rangle = \int v f(v) dv$, and the mean kinetic energy is given by $\langle E \rangle = \frac{1}{2} m \langle v^2 \rangle = \frac{3}{2} kT$. The pressure of ideal gas is the force exerted per unit area by particle collisions, which is given by $P = \frac{1}{3} n m \langle v^2 \rangle = n k T$.  For a highly relativistic gas, the energy is given by $E = pc$, so the pressure is given by $P = \frac{1}{3} n \langle pc \rangle = \frac{1}{3} u$, where $u$ is the energy density. This relativistic particles can be the cosmic rays. Besides, we can also derive the pressure by the equation of state, which is given by $P = (\gamma - 1) u$, where $\gamma$ is the adiabatic index. For non-relativistic gas, $\gamma = 5/3$, while for relativistic gas, $\gamma = 4/3$.


## Radiation


When we say radiation, we mean the photon interact with matter. The important thing is the number of photons might not be conserved, some photon can be abosrbed, some can be re-emitted. In this pircture we assume everything is in thermal equilibrium. We know that for photon gas thry dollow the Planck distribution (Actually it might be Bose distribution). The number of photon states in 3D in ($\vec{k}, \vec{k} + d\vec{k}$) is given by $d^3 q = \frac{L^3} 4 \pi k^2 \frac{d k}{(2\pi)^3}$, where $k = \frac{2 \pi q}{l}$ is the wavenumber. From this we can derive the density of states, which is given by $\frac{d^3 q}{L^3} = \frac{8 \pi \nu^3}{c^3} d \nu$. Finally we can derive the energy density of radiation by $u_{\nu} = \frac{8 \pi h \nu^3}{c^3} \frac{1}{e^{h\nu/kT} - 1}$, and the unit of which is $erg/cm^3/Hz$. The flux of the radiation is then given by $F_{\nu} = \frac{c} u_{\nu}$, and the intensity should divide by the solid angle, which is given by $I_{\nu} = \frac{c}{4\pi} u_{\nu}$. Typically we deal with black body, so we use the notation $B(\nu, T)$ to represent the intensity. The total energy density of the radiation is given by $u = \int u_{\nu} d\nu = a T^4$, the key thing here is the radiation energy density is proportional to the fourth power of the temperature. The intensity is given by $B(T) = \frac{c}{4\pi} a T^4$, and the flux is given by $F = \frac{a c}{4} T^4$. We can deduce from the above equations that $F = \pi B(T)$, this is because we can only see the half of the radiation(hemisphere). Note that the flux here is what we observed, so it is a little different from the previous definition of flux, which is the total flux. 



## Radiative transfer


What is radiative transfer? It is the process of radiation propagating through the medium. The radiation can be absorbed, scattered, or emitted by the medium. We need to define some emission coefficient here first. The emission coefficient is defined by $j_{\nu} = \frac{d I_{\nu}}{d x} $, which is the change of intensity per unit length, the unit of which is $erg/s/cm^3/Hz/steradian$. If we assume the gas is isortopic, we can derive the volume emissivity, which is given by $\epsilon_{\nu} = 4\pi j_{\nu}$, this is most frequently used in astrophysics, which refers to the energy emitted per unit volume per unit time per unit frequency. Besides, we can also define the absorption coefficient, which is defined as $  d I_{\nu} = - \kappa_{\nu} I_{\nu} dx $, where $\kappa_{\nu}$ is the absorption coefficient, which is in unit of $cm^{-1}$. Thus $\kappa$ here describes the fractional absorption or scatter of a radiation beam, per unit length. This has a different unit since we want to describe the fractional absorption inside the medium. We have $ \kappa \approx n \sigma_{\nu} \approx \frac{1}{\lambda_{mfp}} $, where $n$ is the number density of the medium and $\sigma_{\nu}$ is the cross section of the absorption or scattering. When the cross section is large, the absorption is strong, and the mean free path is short. 


With all of these defined, we can finally write down the transfer equation, which is given by $\frac{d I_{\nu}}{d x} = j_{\nu} - \kappa_{\nu} I_{\nu}$. Here we look at one more defintion, that is the optical depth, which is defined by $\tau_{\nu} = \int \kappa_{\nu} dx$, and it is comparable to $\frac{L}{\lambda_{mfp}}$. And we have optically thin/thick medium, which is defined by $\tau \ll 1$ and $\tau \gg 1$ respectively. And if we change the variable from $x$ to $\tau$, we can get the transfer equation in terms of optical depth, which is given by $\frac{d I_{\nu}}{d \tau} = S_{\nu} - I_{\nu}$, where $S_{\nu} = \frac{j_{\nu}}{\kappa_{\nu}}$ is called the source function. We integrate the above equation, and we can get $I_{\nu}(\tau) = I_{\nu}(0) e^{-\tau} + \int S_{\nu} e^{-(\tau - \tau')} d \tau'$. The first term is the attenuation of the background source, and it describes the absorption and scattering of the background source. The second term is the contribution from the medium itself, and it describes the emission from the medium. If we let $I_0 = 0$, ignoring the background source, we can get $I_{\nu}(\tau) = S_{\nu} (1 - e^{-\tau})$. When $\tau \ll 1$, we have $I_{\nu} \approx S_{\nu} \tau = j_{\nu} L$, which means the emission from each element of the medium can be simply added together and get into our eyes. When $\tau \gg 1$, we have $I_{\nu} \approx S_{\nu}$, which means the final intensity is just the source function but it can be quite different from the emissivity, since the absorption and scattering inside the medium is very strong so we cannot see the emissivity directly.


Now we consider the local thermal equilibrium (LTE), which means $\tau \ll 1$. In this case, we have $I_{\nu} \approx S_{\nu} \approx B_{\nu}(T)$, from which we can deduce that $j_{\nu} \approx \kappa_{\nu} B_{\nu}(T)$. This is called the Kirchhoff's law, which means the emission coefficient is connected to the absorption coefficient by the black body intensity. Then we define the so-called brightness temperature, which is defined by $I_{\nu} = B_{\nu}(T_b)$, which means the intensity is equal to the black body intensity at the brightness temperature. For example, for $\nu \ll k_B T$, we have $B_{\nu}(T) \approx \frac{2 k_B T \nu^2}{c^2}$, so we can infer that $T_B = T (1 - e^{ -\tau_{\nu}})$. When $\tau \gg 1$, we have $T_B \approx T $, which means the brightness temperature is close to the physical temperature, while when $\tau \ll 1$, we have $T_B \approx T \tau_{\nu}$, which means the brightness temperature is much smaller than the physical temperature. The example for the first case is GMC, where we have $T \approx T_B \approx 10 K$, and the example for the second case is ISM, where we have $T_B \approx T \tau \approx 10^-2 K $.
